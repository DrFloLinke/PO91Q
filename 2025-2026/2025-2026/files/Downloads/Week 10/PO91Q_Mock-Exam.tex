\section*{Instructions}\label{instructions}
\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{Instructions}

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  Part \ref{part:questions} contains the questions for the exam

  \begin{itemize}
  \tightlist
  \item
    These are divided into four blocks: A, B, C, and D.
  \item
    Block A is compulsory and must be answered by all candidates.
  \item
    You must then select ONE block out of B, C, and D.
  \item
    Should you change your mind during the exam, indicate clearly which
    block you wish to have marked.
  \item
    You can achieve a maximum of 120 points.
  \item
    Information on the maximum number of points obtainable is outlined
    at the beginning of each block
  \end{itemize}

  Part \ref{part:case} contains the case study to which all questions in
  Block A of Part \ref{part:questions} refer
\item
  Part \ref{part:tables} contains the relevant statistical tables

  \begin{itemize}
  \tightlist
  \item
    Normal curve tail probabilities are provided in Table
    \ref{tbl:normal}, page \pageref{tbl:normal}.
  \item
    t-distribution critical values are provided in Table \ref{tbl:t},
    page \pageref{tbl:t}.
  \end{itemize}
\item
  Part \ref{part:notation} contains the formula collection and notation
  of the module

  \begin{itemize}
  \tightlist
  \item
    If you use notation other than in the formula collection (page
    \pageref{tbl:formulae_po11q}) or notation table (page
    \pageref{sec:Notation}), give an explanation.
  \end{itemize}
\item
  Allowed time: 2 hours.
\item
  A university-approved calculator is allowed.
\end{itemize}

\section{Preface}\label{preface}

This case study resembles a shorter version of one you will receive for
the exam.

Today you will be working with the ``Scottish Index of Multiple
Deprivation''. The data are taken from \citet{simd:2020}.

\begin{quote}
The Scottish Index of Multiple Deprivation is a relative measure of
deprivation across 6,976 small areas (called data zones). If an area is
identified as `deprived', this can relate to people having a low income
but it can also mean fewer resources or opportunities. SIMD looks at the
extent to which an area is deprived across seven domains: income,
employment, education, health, access to services, crime and housing. It
ranks data zones from most deprived (ranked 1) to least deprived (ranked
6,976).
\end{quote}

\begin{quote}
SIMD is the Scottish Government's standard approach to identify areas of
multiple deprivation in Scotland. It can help improve understanding
about the outcomes and circumstances of people living in the most
deprived areas in Scotland. It can also allow effective targeting of
policies and funding where the aim is to wholly or partly tackle or take
account of area concentrations of multiple deprivation.
\end{quote}

\begin{quote}
Data zones in rural areas tend to cover a large land area and reflect a
more mixed picture of people experiencing different levels of
deprivation. This means that SIMD is less helpful at identifying the
smaller pockets of deprivation found in more rural areas, compared to
the larger pockets found in urban areas. SIMD domain indicators can
still be useful in rural areas if analysed separately from urban data
zones or combined with other data.
\end{quote}
